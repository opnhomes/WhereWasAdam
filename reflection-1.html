The Gap  ·  Part I  ·  Identity

The Question Nobody Is Asking

On who men are, who God made them to be, and what happens when no one tells them the difference.

There is a gap.

Most men feel it. Few can name it. It shows up as restlessness at 2am, a vague dissatisfaction with the life that looks fine on paper, a sense that something is missing — not from the calendar, not from the bank account, but from somewhere deeper. From the soul.

The question Where was Adam? is not a history question. It is not a theology exam. It is a mirror.

Where are you?

The Original Gap

In Genesis 3, the serpent approaches Eve. Adam is nowhere to be found — or worse, he is there and silent. The first man, given dominion over creation, gifted with a bride, entrusted with the garden — abdicated. He stood by while the enemy deceived his wife and said nothing.

God's response is devastating in its precision. He does not call for Eve first. He does not address the serpent first. He calls out:

"Where are you, Adam?" Genesis 3:9

That question has never stopped echoing.

It echoes in every home where a father is physically present but spiritually absent. It echoes in every marriage where a husband has handed over leadership out of comfort or cowardice. It echoes in every parish where the pews are full of women and the men are somewhere else — anywhere else.

God is still asking. And most men still don't have an answer.

The Identity Crisis Nobody Names

Here is the honest diagnosis: men today do not know who they are.

Not because they are stupid. Not because they are bad. But because the culture that was supposed to form them failed catastrophically — and the Church, in too many places, went quiet at precisely the wrong moment.

A boy becomes a man through initiation — through challenge, suffering, accountability, and blessing from older men who have gone before him. When that chain breaks, boys become older boys. They accumulate possessions, achievements, and relationships — but they never receive the one thing they were waiting for:

Someone to look them in the eye and say — you are a man. Now act like one.

That void gets filled. It always does. Sometimes with work. Sometimes with sports. Sometimes with pornography, alcohol, or distraction. Sometimes with quiet, suffocating despair.

The gap is not a productivity problem. It is an identity problem.

What the Church Has Always Known

The Catholic faith has the answer that no self-help book, no men's retreat built around drum circles, and no motivational podcast can provide.

You are made in the image and likeness of God.

"God created mankind in his image; in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them." Genesis 1:27

This is not a sentiment. It is an ontological declaration. Before a man has done anything — before he has succeeded or failed, before he has been a good husband or a bad one, before he has shown up or checked out — he bears the Imago Dei. The image of God is stamped into his very nature.

But here is where Catholic anthropology goes further than the culture ever could:

The Imago Dei is not just a status. It is a vocation.

To be made in the image of God is to be made for relationship, sacrifice, and mission — because that is who God is. The Trinity itself is a communion of self-giving love. The Father pours Himself into the Son. The Son lays down His life. The Spirit proceeds as the love between them.

A man who does not give himself away is not living his identity. He is hiding from it. Just like Adam.

The Man God Had in Mind

Look at Jesus Christ.

Not the pale, passive figure sometimes presented in bad art and worse homilies. The real Christ — the one who fashioned a whip and drove merchants from the Temple, who spoke with authority that silenced crowds, who wept at the tomb of Lazarus, who sweat blood in Gethsemane and still said not my will but yours.

That is the model of Catholic masculinity. Strength ordered by love. Courage animated by sacrifice. Leadership expressed as service.

St. John Paul II called men to rediscover this vision. In his Theology of the Body he revealed that the human body itself — male and female — is a theology, a visible sign of invisible realities. The male body is made to initiate, to protect, to give. Not to dominate. Not to abdicate. To give.

The gap is what happens when men stop giving.

Closing the Gap

Identity is not found. It is received — and then chosen, daily.

It is received in Baptism, where a man is claimed by God as a beloved son. It is deepened in Confirmation, where he is sealed as a soldier of Christ. It is sustained in the Eucharist, where he is nourished by the Body of the One who shows him what total self-gift looks like.

Every morning a man wakes up and faces the same question Adam faced:

Will you show up — or will you disappear?

Closing the gap begins not with a program, not with a resolution, not with a better morning routine. It begins with one honest answer to the question God has been asking since the garden:

Here I am, Lord.

For Reflection

  • Where in your life have you been silent when you should have spoken?
  • Where have you been present in body but absent in soul?
  • What would it look like to live as a man who knows he bears the image of God?
"You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our hearts are restless until they rest in you." St. Augustine, Confessions
