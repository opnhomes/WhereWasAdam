The Man She Married Is Still in There

On vocation, sacrificial love, and the dignity your wife deserves.

There is a lie so embedded in the culture that most men have stopped noticing it. It runs through advertising, entertainment, pornography, and casual conversation, and it goes like this: women exist, in some fundamental way, for men's pleasure. To be desired. To be acquired. To satisfy.

Most men reading this would say they don't believe that. But belief is revealed by behavior, not by what we say we think. And the question worth sitting with is not whether you've accepted the world's version of this lie — but whether some quieter version of it has taken root inside your marriage.

The quieter version doesn't look like the billboards. It looks like a husband who is physically present but emotionally checked out. Who is kind enough when he's getting what he needs but distant when he's not. Who has — over years, almost imperceptibly — begun relating to his wife in terms of what she provides: comfort, companionship, physical intimacy, a well-run home. She is still loved, in a way. But she is loved the way a man loves something useful.

John Paul II spent years thinking about this before he became pope. In Love and Responsibility, he wrote that the opposite of love is not hate. It is use. To use a person is to treat them as a means to your own ends — to relate to them instrumentally, for what they give you, rather than seeing them as a subject with a soul, a dignity, a destiny that has nothing to do with you.

This is what lust does. Not just the dramatic kind. The ordinary kind. It reduces the person before you to the body before you, and the body to what it can give you. And it is possible to do this to your wife — the woman you stood at an altar with — without ever realizing it's happening.

A Vocation, Not a Lifestyle Choice

The Catechism calls marriage a covenant — not a contract, not a companionship arrangement, not a social institution. A covenant between God, man and woman.

"The matrimonial covenant, by which a man and a woman establish between themselves a partnership of the whole of life, is by its nature ordered toward the good of the spouses and the procreation and education of offspring." CCC 1601

That word — ordered — matters. Marriage isn't neutral. It has a direction. It is pointed toward something: the good of the spouses, and toward life. It is inherently generative. A marriage that is not producing life — in the home, in the spouses themselves, in the children it raises, in the community it serves — is a marriage that is working against its own nature.

And you were called to it. Vocation comes from the Latin vocare — to call. A vocation is not something you chose, but something God called you to. The decision to marry was yours, but the call behind it was God's. He placed this particular woman in front of you. He is asking something of you through her.

The question is whether you're listening.

What Sacrificial Love Actually Looks Like

"Husbands, love your wives as Christ loved the Church and gave himself up for her." Ephesians 5:25

Christ did not love the Church because it was lovable. He loved it into becoming what it was meant to be. His love was not responsive — it was generative. It did not wait for the right conditions. It went first, at great cost, without guarantee of return.

That is the model. Not a husband who is warm when his wife is warm, engaged when she is engaging, present when it's convenient. A husband whose love is the cause of life in his home, not a response to it.

John Paul II's Theology of the Body gives us language for this. The body, he argued, has a nuptial meaning — it is made for total self-gift. The union of husband and wife is meant to be a physical sign of what love actually is: complete, exclusive, faithful, and life-giving. Not a transaction. Not a comfort arrangement. A participation in the kind of love God has for his people.

This is why the Church's teaching on marriage is not a list of prohibitions. It is an invitation to something staggeringly high. The husband who loves his wife sacrificially — who sees her as a person with a soul and a dignity that must be honored — is imitating Christ. He is participating in something sacred.

The Question

So where are you?

Not physically. Every married man reading this is physically somewhere in proximity to his wife. The question is whether you are present — whether she is receiving from you the kind of love that sees her, honors her dignity, and lays something down for her.

The gap in marriage can be dramatic, but more often it is subtle. It doesn't usually open in a single moment. It opens slowly, through a thousand small retreats — the conversation you didn't have, being somewhere else in your mind, or stopped honoring the dignity God gave her.

The man she married is still in there. He just went quiet.

It's time to show up.
